## drag-x
### instruction
Drag the blue x upward to increase it. Watch how log(x) grows slowly compared to x itself.

### hint
Find the blue x in the formula and drag it upward.

### insight
Notice how x went from 20 to a bigger number, but log(x) barely changed? Logarithms grow incredibly slowly. Going from 10 to 100 only adds 1 to the log.

## product-rule
### instruction
Now drag both x and y. Watch the bars: the bottom bar (log of x times y) always equals the sum of the top two bars.

### hint
Change both x and y and compare the three colored bars.

### insight
This is the product rule of logarithms: log(xy) = log(x) + log(y). Multiplication becomes addition! This is why slide rules worked -- engineers could multiply by adding lengths.

## earthquake
### instruction
Set x to 10 and y to 10. Their product is 100, but log(100) = log(10) + log(10) = just 2.

### hint
Set x=10, y=10 and look at the result.

### insight
This is why the Richter scale uses logarithms. An earthquake of magnitude 8 is not "a little bigger" than magnitude 7 -- it's 10 times more powerful. Logarithms let us handle enormous ranges on a human-friendly scale.
